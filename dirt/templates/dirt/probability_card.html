The distribution of the "natural log" of the results doesn't say anything about the total quantity you might find. However the peak of the bell curve tells us where most of results from years in the study fall.

As the peak of the bell curve goes to the right (as x gets bigger) the greater the chance of finding elevated values of trash per meter. What we want to see is the peak of the chart go to the left.

That is clearly not the case for 2016-2017. So that means your chances of finding 1 piece of trash or less per meter in 2017 were about the same in 2016(about 3%).

How is 2018 looking?(hint: take a look at the scatter plot)

The year over year probability of finding trash on the shoreline is unchanged. This could mean that current efforts are keeping up with increases in ecomnomic activity or population density.
